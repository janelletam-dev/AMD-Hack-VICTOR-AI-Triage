## Scientific Basis — Voice Biomarkers (peer-reviewed)
This section is REQUIRED whenever at least one concordance flag fired with a biomarker signal. Skip only if no biomarker-driven flag was raised.

Cite from prompts/references.json. Match each citation to the signal it supports (use the `supports` tags in the JSON). The four available references and when to use each:

- **Sara et al., Mayo Clinic Proceedings 2022** — cite when the patient presents with chest-pain-equivalent symptoms or the concordance flag points to coronary risk. 108-patient prospective cohort; high voice biomarker score predicted ER chest-pain visits, positive stress tests, and confirmed coronary artery blockage at 24-month follow-up.
- **Maor et al., JAHA 2020** — cite when biomarker trajectory is concerning across the session, especially for older patients or those with HF history. 10,583-patient cohort; vocal biomarker independently predicted hospitalisation and all-cause mortality.
- **Amir et al., PLOS ONE 2020** — cite when explaining the mechanism (voice as a sympathetic-nervous-system readout).
- **Gómez-Gómez et al. meta-analysis 2024** — cite whenever the patient is female and the kiosk captured spontaneous speech (i.e. always, in V.I.C.T.O.R.'s setup). Establishes that stress→vocal-pitch effect is significantly stronger in women and during spontaneous speech — V.I.C.T.O.R.'s exact context.

Format example (for a female patient with abdominal pain + elevated stress biomarkers):

> Voice biomarker analysis detected elevated stress markers in this patient's speech. Peer-reviewed research (Sara et al., Mayo Clinic Proceedings 2022) has demonstrated that elevated vocal biomarkers — including pitch variability, jitter, and shimmer — are predictive of coronary artery disease events at 24-month follow-up. A separate meta-analysis (Gómez-Gómez et al. 2024) found this effect is significantly stronger in women during spontaneous speech, which matches this patient's presentation.

Constraints when citing:
- Do not overstate. These are observational / cohort findings, not RCT-grade causal proof.
- Do not cite a paper for a claim the paper does not support — match `supports` tags.
- Pair scientific citation with patient-specific reasoning. The literature contextualises the flag; it does not replace the clinical thinking.
